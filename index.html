Welcome everyone. Today I want to challenge something fundamental about how we practice echocardiography. This isn't about a new technique or a new device - it's about the mathematical foundations we use every single day. By the end of this presentation, I hope to convince you that our current normalization methods are deeply flawed, and that we have the tools to do better.

A Simple Question

Patient Profile:

  • Female patient
  • BSA: 1.8 m²
  • Following Strom 2024 reference values...

Indexed Values (mean):

  • IVS/BSA = 0.60 cm/m²
  • LVPW/BSA = 0.51 cm/m²
  • LVIDd/BSA = 2.40 cm/m²

All within normal range. What would you expect for LV mass?

Let's start with a patient. Female, BSA 1.8 m². We're using the Strom 2024 reference values. IVS indexed to BSA is 0.60. LVPW is 0.51. LVIDd is 2.40. All of these are within the normal reference range. Before we go further, I want you to think: based on these measurements, what would you expect for LV mass?

Calculate the LV Mass

ASE LV Mass Formula:

LV Mass = 0.8 × [1.04 × (IVS + LVIDd + LVPW)³ - (LVIDd)³] + 0.6

Converting our indexed values back to absolute measurements:

  • IVS = 0.60 × 1.8 = 1.08 cm
  • LVPW = 0.51 × 1.8 = 0.92 cm
  • LVIDd = 2.40 × 1.8 = 4.32 cm

Take a moment to think...

Larger walls + larger diameter = ?

Here's the ASE formula we all use. It's pure geometry - we cube the sum of these measurements. When we convert our indexed values back to absolute measurements for a BSA of 1.8, we get IVS of 1.08 cm, LVPW of 0.92 cm, and LVIDd of 4.32 cm. Larger walls plus larger diameter - geometrically, what should happen to mass? Let the audience think about this before advancing.

The Contradiction

⚠️ The Reveal

According to Strom's linear measurements:

Females should have LARGER LV mass

But Strom's published LV mass reference says:

Males have LARGER LV mass

Same data. Same patient. Opposite conclusions.

This isn't:

  • ❌ A data problem
  • ❌ A measurement error
  • ✅ A mathematical problem with how we normalize measurements
Here's the reveal. The same Strom reference data says that females should have SMALLER indexed LV mass than males. Same data. Same patient. Opposite conclusions. The linear measurements tell us females should have larger mass. The published mass values tell us males should have larger mass. This is mathematically impossible. And this isn't a data problem or a measurement error. This is a fundamental mathematical problem with ratiometric scaling. To understand why this happens, we need to look at what ratiometric scaling actually assumes.

The Simple Ratio

Indexed Value = Measurement / BSA

This is what we do every day:

  • Seems logical, right?
  • Normalize for body size
  • Make patients comparable

But let's look deeper...

This is the simple ratio we use constantly. Measurement divided by BSA. It seems logical - we're normalizing for body size. But there's a hidden assumption here that we need to unpack.

The Hidden Assumption

Rearranging the formula:

Measurement = Indexed Value × BSA
[Graph: Line through origin]
X-axis: BSA (m²)
Y-axis: Measurement
Slope = Indexed Value

This is a line through the origin

  • Assumes a linear relationship
  • Assumes if BSA = 0, measurement = 0
  • Assumes the same slope works for everyone
When we rearrange this formula, we see what we're really saying: Measurement equals indexed value times BSA. This is a line through the origin. It assumes a perfectly linear relationship. It assumes that if BSA equals zero, the measurement equals zero. And it assumes the same slope - the same indexed value - works for everyone. This can only be accurate at ONE point - usually calibrated to the population average. Everyone else gets systematically biased.

But Biology Isn't Linear

[Scatter plot: IVS vs BSA]
Shows actual curved relationship
Overlay: Forced linear fit through origin
Regions labeled "Overestimated" and "Underestimated"

Key insights:

  • The heart doesn't go to zero at BSA = 0
  • Newborns have hearts!
  • The relationship is a curve, not a line
  • When we force a line onto a curve, we create bias
Here's the problem: biology isn't linear. The heart doesn't go to zero when BSA equals zero - newborns have hearts! The real relationship is a curve, not a line. When we force a straight line through the origin onto a curved relationship, we systematically overestimate some patients and underestimate others. And this bias is not random - it's systematic and predictable.

Small patients (low BSA)

  • Measurements appear falsely elevated
  • May be labeled abnormal when normal

Large patients (high BSA)

  • Measurements appear falsely reduced
  • May miss true abnormalities

Patients far from average BMI

  • Athletes
  • Obesity
  • Eating disorders
  • Elderly with sarcopenia
  • Anyone with unusual body composition

So who gets hurt by this? Small patients with low BSA - their measurements appear falsely elevated. They might be labeled abnormal when they're actually normal. Large patients with high BSA - the opposite. Measurements appear falsely reduced. We might miss true abnormalities. And patients far from average BMI - athletes, obesity, eating disorders, sarcopenia - anyone with unusual body composition. These are often the patients who need the most careful assessment. And they're the ones getting the worst bias from our normalization methods.

This Isn't Just Strom Data

Tricuspid Valve Contradictions

Reference: Addetia et al., JACC Img 2019

The Contradiction:

  • Females: larger tricuspid perimeter/BSA
  • Males: larger tricuspid area/BSA

Why This Is Impossible:

Perimeter and area are mathematically related:

Area ∝ Perimeter²

For a given shape, if perimeter is larger, area must be larger.

These cannot show opposite relationships.

Now, this isn't just the Strom data. These contradictions appear across multiple studies. Look at the tricuspid valve data from Addetia 2019. Females have larger tricuspid perimeter when indexed by BSA. But males have larger tricuspid area when indexed by BSA. This is geometrically impossible. Perimeter and area are mathematically related. Area is proportional to perimeter squared. If perimeter is larger, area must be larger. These cannot show opposite relationships. This is the same scaling artifact we saw with LV mass.

Stroke Volume Paradox

Reference: Patel et al., JASE 2021

The Contradiction:

  • Females: larger LVOT diameter/BSA
  • Females: larger VTI/BSA
  • Yet...
  • Females: SMALLER stroke volume/BSA ❓

Why This Is Impossible:

SV = (π/4) × D² × VTI

If D is larger AND VTI is larger...

SV must be larger

The math doesn't work.

Here's another example from the WASE stroke volume data. Females have larger LVOT diameter when indexed by BSA. Females have larger VTI when indexed by BSA. Yet females have SMALLER stroke volume when indexed by BSA. This is impossible. Stroke volume equals pi over 4 times diameter squared times VTI. If both diameter and VTI are larger, stroke volume must be larger. The math doesn't work. Same problem, different parameter, same root cause.

Let's recognize the pattern here. These aren't random errors or isolated mistakes. They're systematic. They appear across multiple studies, different parameters, different authors, different populations. They all stem from the same root cause: ratiometric scaling. Forcing nonlinear biological relationships into linear mathematical models. Using inappropriate normalization methods. And they have real clinical consequences. They affect our decision-making. They create sex-based biases. They penalize patients with unusual body habitus. They reduce diagnostic accuracy. So when does this actually matter clinically?

5. Guideline-Based Decisions

Many guidelines use indexed values → Medication initiation → Intervention timing

So when does this matter clinically? When we're determining LVH - cutoff values are based on indexed mass. Different methods give different diagnoses. Assessing chamber enlargement - dilated versus normal triggers completely different management. Pediatric and congenital Z-scores - critical for surgical timing. Serial measurements when body size changes - is the heart changing or is it indexing artifact? And guideline-based decisions - medications, interventions, all based on indexed values. Clinical Scenario 1

The Tall, Thin Patient

Patient Profile

  • 30-year-old male
  • Height: 195 cm (6'5")
  • Weight: 75 kg (165 lbs)
  • BMI: 19.7 (underweight)
  • BSA: 2.05 m²

Measurements

  • IVS: 1.2 cm
  • LVIDd: 5.4 cm
  • LVPW: 1.1 cm
  • LV Mass: 185 g

Using BSA Indexing

LV Mass/BSA = 185 / 2.05 = 90 g/m²

Below male cutoff of 115 g/m²

✓ "Normal"

But consider...

  • Very thin (BMI 19.7)
  • Muscle mass likely low
  • Is 185g really normal for this body composition?
  • Using height^2.7: indexed value would be higher

Possible missed early cardiomyopathy?

Here's a real scenario. Tall, thin 30-year-old male. 6'5", 165 pounds. BMI 19.7 - underweight. BSA 2.05 - large BSA driven by his height. Echo shows IVS 1.2, diameter 5.4, posterior wall 1.1. Calculated mass 185 grams. Using BSA indexing, that's 90 grams per meter squared. Below the male cutoff of 115. We call it normal. But is it? He's very thin. Low muscle mass. Is 185 grams really normal for his body composition? Using a height-based method, the indexed value would be higher. Might appear abnormal. Might be early cardiomyopathy. Different indexing method, different diagnosis.

The Precision Medicine Paradox

What We CAN Do

  • Sequence entire genomes in hours
  • Use AI for personalized risk prediction
  • Measure global longitudinal strain to 0.1%
  • 3D-print patient-specific models
  • Identify genetic variants affecting drug metabolism

What We DON'T Do

  • Normalize measurements appropriately for body size
  • Account for body composition
  • Use dimensionally sound scaling
Measurement / BSA

A simple ratio from the 1800s

Our tools have evolved.
Our scaling methods haven't.
Here's the paradox we face in precision medicine. We can sequence entire genomes in hours. We use AI for personalized risk prediction. We measure strain to a tenth of a percent. We 3D-print patient-specific models. We identify genetic variants that affect drug metabolism. Yet for body size normalization, we use a simple ratio from the 1800s. We don't account for body composition. We don't use dimensionally sound scaling. Our tools have evolved. Our scaling methods haven't. But better approaches exist - and they're being validated right now.

Better Approaches Exist

What Works?

1. Allometric Scaling (Dimensional Analysis)

The Principle:

  • Linear measurements ~ BSA^0.5
  • Areas ~ BSA^1.0
  • Volumes and mass ~ BSA^1.5

Why It Works:

  • Respects geometric relationships
  • Based on physical dimensions
  • You saw it work in the lab (⭐ methods)

Formula Examples:

Linear: Measurement / BSA^0.5
Mass: Measurement / BSA^1.5

Evidence:

  • Reduces contradictions
  • Better across body size range
  • Mathematically sound

2. Multivariable Prediction Models

The Principle:

  • Don't use ratios at all
  • Build regression models with multiple predictors
  • Adjust for age, sex, ethnicity, BSA simultaneously

Example:

Shiwani et al. 2024 (JACC)

  • Personalized LVH thresholds
  • Accounts for demographics
  • More accurate risk prediction

Advantages:

  • Captures complex relationships
  • No forced linearity
  • Can include relevant covariates
  • Individualized assessment

3. Properly Constructed Z-scores

The Principle:

  • Model the entire distribution
  • Account for multiple body size metrics
  • Allow nonlinear relationships

Example:

Lauzon-Schnittka et al. 2025

  • Pediatric echo Z-scores
  • Adjusts for BSA, BMI, and age
  • Appropriate statistical modeling

Advantages:

  • Individual-level assessment
  • Captures growth patterns
  • Accounts for variability
  • Already proven in pediatrics
So what works? First, allometric scaling - dimensional analysis. Linear measurements scale with BSA to the 0.5 power. Mass scales with BSA to the 1.5 power. Respects geometric relationships. Based on physical dimensions. You saw it work in the lab - those were the star methods. Second, multivariable prediction models. Don't use ratios at all. Build regression models that adjust for age, sex, ethnicity, BSA simultaneously. Shiwani 2024 showed this works for personalized LVH thresholds. Third, properly constructed Z-scores. Model the entire distribution. Account for multiple body size metrics. Allow nonlinear relationships. Lauzon-Schnittka 2025 did this for pediatric echo. Already proven to work.
